# Silk Road Reconstructed

## About the Project

For this assignment, I wanted to create an interactive experience that explores history through participation rather than observation.

While researching museum and history websites, I noticed that users are often expected to read information, browse collections, or move through archives. Although these websites contain valuable content, the experience can sometimes feel passive.

This led me to a simple question:

How could interaction help people understand history rather than simply read about it?

This question became the starting point for the project.

To explore this idea, I developed *Silk Road Reconstructed*, a browser-based interactive prototype inspired by the Silk Road.

The chosen action for this project is **Drag**. Rather than using drag as a simple interface mechanic, I wanted it to become part of the concept itself. Users physically move, rebuild and connect historical objects, turning interaction into a process of reconstruction and discovery.

---

## Why the Silk Road?

I chose the Silk Road because I was interested in the idea of connection.

When people think about the Silk Road, they often imagine a single trade route connecting East and West. However, during my research I discovered that it was much more than that. It was a network through which goods, technologies, religions, knowledge and cultural practices travelled between different regions.

This idea of exchange became the foundation of the project.

Rather than focusing on one historical event, I wanted users to gradually uncover relationships between objects and discover how those relationships formed larger systems of exchange.

## Context and Interaction Design

The historical context directly influenced the visual style, usability and feedback systems used throughout the project.

### Style

The visual design was inspired by historical maps, archaeological fragments and museum displays.

Warm colours, aged textures and route-based graphics were used to create a sense of historical exploration.

These visual choices were influenced by historical maps, museum displays and archaeological artefacts encountered during my research.

The interface was designed to feel like a collection of recovered artefacts rather than a modern website. This visual direction helps reinforce the idea that users are reconstructing fragments of history.

### Usability

Because the project focuses on reconstruction, I wanted the interaction to feel simple and direct.

I experimented with clicking, hovering and scrolling, but drag felt the most appropriate. Dragging allows users to physically move, repair and connect objects, making the historical content easier to understand through action rather than large amounts of text.

This interaction also supports the project's core concept. Users do not simply access information; they actively participate in rebuilding it.

### Feedback

Feedback was designed to support discovery.

Visual highlights, glowing artefacts, route animations and progress indicators help users understand when meaningful historical connections have been found.

Rather than presenting information immediately, the project rewards interaction by gradually revealing historical narratives and routes across the map.

These feedback systems help guide users through the experience while maintaining a sense of exploration.

---

## Design Process

The project is divided into three stages.

### Phase 1 – Reconstruction

The experience begins with fragmented artefacts.

Users drag pieces together to restore complete objects.

This interaction was inspired by archaeological reconstruction, where historians and archaeologists often work with fragments rather than complete objects. My goal was to encourage users to actively participate in rebuilding history before they are introduced to larger historical ideas.

### Phase 2 – Connection

Once the artefacts have been reconstructed, users enter a map-based environment.

Here, objects can be dragged together to reveal historical relationships.

Each successful pairing unlocks a different narrative about exchange along the Silk Road.

Examples include:

* Spices + Coin → Economic Exchange
* Coin + Ceramic → Trade Networks
* Ceramic + Silk → Cultural Exchange
* Silk + Manuscript → Spread of Ideas
* Manuscript + Compass → Knowledge and Navigation

This stage shifts the focus from individual artefacts to relationships between them.

### Phase 3 – Reflection

As users discover more connections, routes across the map become illuminated.

The final stage presents the Silk Road as a network rather than a single road.

This was important because one of the key ideas behind the project is that history is created through relationships, movement and exchange.

---

## Challenges

One of the biggest challenges was balancing historical content with interaction design.

At first I included too much information, which made the experience feel more like reading a website than interacting with one. Reducing the amount of text allowed the interaction itself to communicate the historical concepts more effectively.

Another challenge was creating interactions that felt meaningful rather than decorative.

Throughout development I kept asking whether each interaction contributed to the idea of reconstruction and discovery. This helped me simplify several design decisions and focus on the core concept.

---

## Future Development

If I continue developing this project, I would like to expand the historical content and introduce additional artefacts, routes and stories.

I would also like to introduce richer historical narratives, improved visual transitions and environmental sound design to create a more immersive experience.

One potential advantage of expanding the project is that the interaction framework could easily support additional historical networks, objects and narratives.

A future challenge would be maintaining usability while increasing historical complexity. As more information is added, careful design decisions would be needed to ensure the experience remains accessible and engaging.
